Engineering Journey

Week 1

PrintCity Induction & First Print

The project began with mandatory printer induction at PrintCity led by Gary Buller. The session introduced machine operation, material handling, QR scan-before-printing workflow, and additive manufacturing practices.

The very next day, the first boglowttom case was printed in black PLA. Although the geometry was acceptable, the dark material made edges, surface quality and dimensional details difficult to inspect. Under PrintCity lighting conditions, identifying layer inconsistencies and dimensional defects became challenging during visual inspection.

This became the first important manufacturing lesson of the project: material and colour selection directly influence quality assurance and inspection capability. Because of this, white PLA was adopted for all future iterations to improve visibility of edges, tolerances and support removal quality.

The induction and first print also changed my understanding of additive manufacturing. The workflow was not simply about operating a printer, but understanding geometry, tolerances, slicing parameters and manufacturability before starting production.

Week 2

Fusion 360 CAD Development

The bottom case was designed quickly using the instructor-provided workflow. However, the top case required interpreting engineering drawings independently and reconstructing the geometry directly inside Fusion 360.

Initial versions intentionally excluded the snap-fit mechanism to validate the primary geometry before introducing tolerance-sensitive features. This reduced manufacturing variables during the early development stages and allowed dimensional verification of the core geometry first.

The top case required interpreting engineering drawings independently and rebuilding the geometry inside Fusion 360. This stage highlighted that dimensions which appear correct in CAD do not always translate directly into successful physical assemblies after printing.

After presenting the early iterations to the instructor, positive feedback on the rapid development progress provided confidence to continue the iterative manufacturing approach throughout the project.

Weeks 3–5

Parameter Optimisation & Print Iterations

Around 30 print iterations were produced across Ultimaker S3, Ultimaker S5 and Bambu Lab P1S printers. Parameters including infill density, layer resolution, print speed and support structures were continuously adjusted.

From Week 4 onwards, PrintCity became part of my daily routine. Even on lecture days starting at 9 AM, I would arrive around 8 AM to begin prints before attending classes on Mondays, Tuesdays and Fridays. This allowed long-duration prints to run during lectures and maximised machine usage throughout the week.

Several iterations initially failed to snap correctly into the top and bottom master components due to excessive interference between mating surfaces. Physical testing revealed that even small dimensional deviations caused significant assembly issues, particularly around the snap-fit geometry and edge clearances. Different print orientations were also evaluated to reduce support interaction, improve dimensional consistency and minimise deformation during printing.

The iterative process gradually shifted from simply producing printable geometry toward Design for Manufacturing principles. Features that appeared acceptable in CAD sometimes failed during physical testing, demonstrating the difference between digital design and real manufacturing performance.

Weeks 6–7

QR Code Traceability & Digital Twin

A QR code traceability system was embedded directly into the top case using dual extrusion printing.

The QR code linked directly to a digital manufacturing record PDF, creating a component-level digital twin system.

This transformed the printed component from a simple physical part into a traceable cyber-physical product aligned with Industry 4.0 manufacturing principles. Each manufactured case could be connected directly to its digital manufacturing data and production history.

Embedding the QR geometry introduced additional manufacturing complexity. Small feature dimensions, dual-colour alignment and surface quality significantly affected QR readability after printing.

Week 8

FESTO CP Lab Validation

Final iterations were tested on the FESTO CP Lab automated assembly line. The measuring station validated dimensional accuracy, while the muscle press station verified snap-fit assembly behaviour.

Week 8 represented the transition from prototyping toward automated manufacturing validation. Several cases successfully passed individual stations, while others failed due to excessive tightness, loose tolerances or inconsistent snap-fit engagement.

The automated system exposed issues that were not immediately visible during manual inspection. Some parts appeared acceptable by hand but failed under automated pressing conditions, highlighting the importance of dimensional consistency within cyber-physical manufacturing systems.

Each tested case was physically marked and analysed after running through the conveyor system to identify which geometries and parameter combinations produced the most reliable manufacturing performance.

Weeks 9

Final Recovery & Submission

An unexpected setback occurred shortly before submission when several zipped bags containing physical print iterations were accidentally left on public transport while travelling to PrintCity. The lost parts represented weeks of iterative manufacturing work.

With only two days remaining before the deadline, the focus shifted from experimentation toward rapid recovery and decision-making. Previously gained manufacturing knowledge was used to prioritise the most successful geometries, tolerances and slicing parameters.

Instead of restarting the entire development cycle, the final validated cases were rebuilt using lessons learned throughout earlier failures, testing and FESTO validation stages.

Engineering Challenges & Process Failures

The project involved continuous manufacturing failures, dimensional inconsistencies and process limitations. Each failed iteration contributed toward understanding the relationship between geometry, tolerances, material behaviour and automated assembly performance.

Dimensional Inconsistency

Identical print parameters often produced different snap-fit behaviour. Some assemblies were excessively tight, while others failed due to loose engagement.

Surface Finish Issues

Poor surface finish affected assembly quality and visual inspection. Black PLA made edge definition difficult to evaluate during early iterations.

Support Removal Problems

Some support structures bonded aggressively to the part, damaging surfaces during removal and affecting dimensional accuracy around critical snap-fit regions.

QR Code Manufacturing Constraints

Embedding a scannable QR code within limited geometry introduced printing complexity, particularly for small feature dimensions and dual-colour extrusion alignment.

Printer Behaviour Variation

Different printers demonstrated different strengths. Some handled QR geometry effectively while struggling with inclined surfaces and overhang regions.

Final Submission Recovery

Multiple bags containing physical iterations were lost shortly before submission, requiring rapid rebuilding of validated parts using previously gained process knowledge.

Value Stream Analysis

A simplified Value Stream Analysis was performed to evaluate process flow efficiency, manufacturing delays and bottlenecks throughout the additive manufacturing workflow. The analysis showed that printing duration represented the largest portion of total manufacturing lead time, while downstream FESTO validation stages remained comparatively underutilised.

Additional delays were introduced during support removal, dimensional inspection and print handling between stations. These findings aligned closely with the simulation-based throughput analysis performed later in the project.

Throughput Analysis

Experiment Manager simulations demonstrated stable production output under varying workforce conditions. Increasing worker count showed negligible improvement because the printing stage constrained system throughput.

Theory of Constraints

Simulation results validated Theory of Constraints principles. The manufacturing system performance was governed primarily by printer cycle time rather than downstream assembly operations.

Printer Utilisation

Ultimaker and Bambu Lab printers operated as the dominant production resources within the simulated factory environment. Faster print cycles significantly improved production capability.

Operator Utilisation

Additional operators did not significantly improve throughput, confirming that labour was not the critical manufacturing constraint within the proposed production system.

Lessons Learned & Engineering Insights

The project demonstrated that additive manufacturing is not simply a printing process, but a complete engineering workflow involving geometry interpretation, material behaviour, manufacturing constraints, tolerance management and automated system integration.

01

Identical Parameters ≠ Identical Output

One of the most important observations was that identical printer settings could still produce different dimensional results. Manufacturing variability became highly visible during snap-fit validation on the FESTO CP Lab system.

02

Automation Exposes Design Weaknesses

Manual assembly could sometimes compensate for poor tolerances, but automated pressing systems immediately exposed dimensional inaccuracies and inconsistent fit behaviour.

03

Print Orientation Impacts Functionality

Geometry orientation strongly affected surface finish, support removal quality and QR readability. Manufacturing decisions directly influenced final usability.

04

Simulation Validated Manufacturing Theory

Tecnomatix simulation demonstrated that increasing labour resources did not improve throughput. Printer cycle time governed the entire production system.

05

Design for Manufacturing Matters

CAD geometry alone does not guarantee manufacturability. Successful production required balancing geometry, tolerances, printability and automated assembly behaviour.

06

Iteration Builds Engineering Knowledge

Failed prints, damaged supports and unsuccessful assemblies became critical learning stages that improved final manufacturing decisions and process understanding.
