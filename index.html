01 — INDUSTRY CHALLENGE

The Phones-R-Us Brief

A client called Phones-R-Us required a manufacturing supplier to design, produce and validate phone casings through a fully automated assembly line — at a commercial scale of 50,000 units per year.

"This was not a design exercise. It was a manufacturing challenge — with real quality gates, real tolerances, and a real production target."

Why 3D Printing Over Injection Moulding?

Injection moulding produces parts in seconds at enormous scale — but requires expensive steel moulds costing tens of thousands of pounds. Any design change requires a completely new mould.

For an iterative development process where snap-fit tolerances needed continuous adjustment across 50+ iterations, FDM 3D printing was the only viable approach. Zero tooling cost. Design changes in minutes. Physical parts within hours.

02 — ENGINEERING JOURNEY

Weekly Development Timeline

From first print to final validation — a nine-week engineering journey documented in full.

Week 1

PrintCity Induction & First Print

The project began with a mandatory printer induction at PrintCity, MMU's dedicated additive manufacturing facility, led by Gary Buller. The session introduced machine operation across Ultimaker S3, S5 and Bambu Lab P1S, material handling, the QR scan-before-printing protocol, and bed preparation fundamentals.

The very next day, the first bottom case was printed in black PLA on the Ultimaker S5. Although the overall geometry was acceptable, the dark material made edges, surface quality, and dimensional details extremely difficult to inspect under lab lighting. White PLA was adopted for all future iterations.

Key Insight "Material colour is not a cosmetic choice — it is a process decision. Inspectability is a quality requirement."
Figure 1 — First black PLA bottom case printed during Week 1 on the Ultimaker S5 at PrintCity.
Week 2

Fusion 360 CAD Development & Engineering Drawings

Both cases were designed in Autodesk Fusion 360. The bottom case was developed quickly using an instructor-provided guided workflow. The top case required independent interpretation of the engineering drawing — including complex curved radii with offset sketch centres that could not be constructed as standard fillets.

Initial versions intentionally excluded the snap-fit mechanism to validate primary geometry before introducing tolerance-sensitive features. Instructor Raul Ochoa Cabrero acknowledged the early progress positively. The snap-fit was left for later iterations — solving too many variables simultaneously would make it impossible to isolate the source of any failure.

Figure 2 — Engineering drawings and Fusion 360 CAD development for top and bottom phone cases.
Weeks 3–5

Parameter Optimisation — The Daily 8am Mission

From this point, PrintCity was visited every morning at 8am — before 9am lectures. On lecture days, a print was started at 8am, classes attended, then results collected and inspected after. This rhythm — print, lecture, inspect, adjust, repeat — was sustained for six weeks.

More than 50 iterations were produced across three printers. Seven parameters were systematically varied: layer resolution, infill density (10–40%), print speed (40–50 mm/s), top surface skin layers (1–3), top surface skin speed (20–40 mm/s), brim width (10–30mm), and ironing. Ironing was permanently disabled after it consistently worsened surface quality rather than improving it.

One major challenge was printer inconsistency — the same machine, same settings, same geometry could produce measurably different results on different days due to temperature fluctuation, bed levelling drift, and ambient lab conditions.

Figure 3 — Ultimaker S5 (UM5 05) at PrintCity — the primary production machine for all final iterations.
Weeks 6–7

QR Code Traceability & Component-Level Digital Twin

After many iterations, tracking which part used which parameters became unmanageable. A traceability solution was needed — but it had to be completely non-intrusive, with no impact on the external geometry, assembly interface, or FESTO performance.

The solution: a QR code embedded in the interior cavity of the top case, printed using the Ultimaker S5's dual extrusion capability — white PLA for the case body, black PLA for the QR modules. The QR code links directly to a Digital Part Record PDF containing complete manufacturing data: Part ID (MFA-DM-030), Version (V30.0), printer, material, temperatures, infill, print duration (3h 21min), weight (27g), and quality verification results.

The bottom case used an embossed batch number and student ID (BATCH 30 / 25937922) — a simpler traceability approach where QR code geometry was less feasible due to the interior surface complexity.

Digital Twin "Every physical part now has a digital identity — scan the QR code and access its complete manufacturing history in seconds."
Figure 4 — Dual-colour QR code embedded in top case interior — white PLA body, black PLA QR modules — linking to the Digital Part Record.
Week 8

FESTO CP Lab Validation Testing

A selection of the best iterations were brought to the FESTO CP Lab for full six-station validation. Each part was marked before testing and results recorded per station. Only two of the six stations performed critical pass/fail evaluation — the Measuring Station (laser dimensional check) and the Muscle Press Station (snap-fit assembly force validation).

Several parts passed the Measuring Station but failed at the Muscle Press — either too tight (assembly rejected) or too loose (no audible engagement click). The differences between passing and failing parts were measured in fractions of a millimetre of snap-fit clearance.

Figure 5 — FESTO CP Lab automated assembly line — six stations, 90-second run time, two critical pass/fail stations.
Week 9

The Bus Incident & Final Recovery

Shortly before the 40% submission deadline, three to four bags containing nearly every physical print from the entire project were accidentally left on the bus and never recovered. Every tested iteration. Every FESTO-passing part. Weeks of physical output — gone.

With two days remaining, the final production parts were rebuilt from scratch using the accumulated process knowledge developed over six weeks. Two intensive days of printing on the dedicated Ultimaker S5 (UM5 05) produced new parts that passed both critical FESTO stations, snapped correctly to the master parts with the correct audible click, and carried full traceability.

Key Lesson "The bags took the prints. They could not take what had been learned. Knowledge and process understanding are more durable than any physical artefact."

04 — PROCESS FAILURES

Engineering Challenges & Process Failures

The project involved continuous manufacturing failures, dimensional inconsistencies and process limitations. Each failed iteration contributed toward understanding the relationship between geometry, tolerances, material behaviour and automated assembly performance.

01

Dimensional Inconsistency

Identical print parameters often produced different snap-fit behaviour across sessions. Some assemblies were excessively tight, while others failed due to loose engagement — caused by temperature fluctuation, bed drift, and filament moisture.

02

Surface Finish Issues

Poor surface finish affected assembly quality and visual inspection. Black PLA made edge definition impossible to evaluate during early iterations. High print speed degraded quality around curved geometry and snap-fit edges.

03

Support Removal Problems

Some support structures bonded aggressively to the part, damaging surfaces during removal and affecting dimensional accuracy around critical snap-fit regions. Dual-material supports were tested as a mitigation strategy.

04

QR Code Manufacturing

Embedding a scannable QR code within constrained geometry introduced printing complexity. Small module dimensions (1–2mm) at print scale required precise speed, offset calibration, and purge volume control across dual extrusion. Multiple iterations before reliable scannability achieved.

05

Printer Behaviour Variation

Different printers demonstrated different strengths. Some handled QR geometry effectively while struggling with inclined surfaces and overhang regions. The Bambu Lab P1S offered speed but could not perform dual extrusion for traceability work.

06

Final Submission Recovery

All physical iterations lost on public transport two days before deadline. Final parts completely rebuilt from accumulated process knowledge in 48 hours — passing all FESTO stations and achieving interchangeability with master parts.

07 — QUALITY VALIDATION

FESTO CP Lab Validation

All parts were tested through the FESTO CP Lab — a modular automated assembly line with six stations. Only two stations performed critical pass/fail evaluation. The remaining four processed and moved parts without assessment.

"The muscle press station was the final boss. It either clicked — or it didn't. There was no middle ground."
Processing Only
01

Magazine Station

Stores and dispenses phone cases from a stacking magazine onto the conveyor pallet. Parts processed automatically without dimensional assessment.

★ Critical — Pass/Fail
02

Measuring Station

Two laser distance sensors check part dimensions at specified measuring points. Parts outside tolerance are rejected. Dimensional accuracy of both cases was critical to passing this station.

Processing Only
03

Pick-by-Light Station

Guided manual assembly station with operator lighting. Integrates manual sequences into the automated line without dimensional checking.

★ Critical — Pass/Fail
04

Muscle Press Station

Presses top case onto bottom case using proportional pressure regulation. Force precisely measured. Too tight — rejected. Too loose — failed retention. The snap-fit tolerance had to be exact to the fraction of a millimetre.

Processing Only
05

Tunnel Furnace

Tempering station operating up to 80°C. Simulates post-assembly thermal processing. Parts processed automatically after successful snap-fit assembly.

Processing Only
06

Workpiece Discharge

Two-axis handling system ejects completed assemblies onto roller conveyors. Final station confirming a complete successful run through the production line.

"Week 9 — two days before submission, all physical parts were lost on the bus. The knowledge was not. Final parts were rebuilt in 48 hours and passed every station."

09 — FINAL REFLECTION

Lessons Learnt

From the first black PLA print that failed inspection to the final white case that snapped perfectly through the FESTO line — this project was a complete engineering education compressed into nine weeks.

✓

What Worked

  • Starting early — physical parts in hand by Week 2
  • Systematic parameter documentation across 50+ iterations
  • QR code traceability — original innovation solving a real problem
  • Incremental snap-fit adjustment — 0.1mm steps, test every version
  • Dedicated printer (UM5 05) for consistency
  • Tecnomatix simulation with real-world failure modelling
  • Cross-verification of parallel vs individual station models
✗

What Was Hard

  • Printer inconsistency — same settings, different results daily
  • Snap-fit tolerance — fractions of mm determined pass or fail
  • QR code dual extrusion — multiple iterations before scannable
  • Losing all parts on the bus two days before deadline
  • 80-object software licence limit preventing full 40-printer model
  • Support removal damaging critical surface regions
→

What I Would Do Differently

  • Implement traceability from Week 1, not Week 6
  • Start Tecnomatix simulation in parallel with printing
  • Document every iteration with photos from day one
  • Never carry all physical parts in one bag
  • Run FESTO validation from Week 5, not Week 8
  • Apply PFMEA thinking before printing, not after failures
"The bags on the bus took the prints. They could not take what had been learned. Knowledge and process understanding are more durable than any physical artefact. That is what engineering actually means."
